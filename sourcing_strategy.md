# Sourcing Strategy — Finding More Federer Companies
## How to Expand Beyond the Initial 25

---

## Overview

The 25 companies in this dataset were identified through manual research across a defined set of sources. This document explains how to systematically find more Federer Company candidates — both within Gujarat and across other Indian states — using a structured multi-source approach.

The goal of a sourcing strategy is not to find all companies. It is to find the right companies efficiently, at the highest possible precision-to-effort ratio.

---

## Source 1 — DSIR-Recognised R&D Companies

**What it is:** The Department of Scientific and Industrial Research (DSIR) maintains a publicly available list of companies with government-recognised in-house R&D centres.

**Why it helps:** DSIR recognition is a strong proxy for technical differentiation (C3). Companies that have cleared the DSIR process have demonstrated documented R&D capabilities, dedicated research staff, and structured IP generation. This immediately separates technical builders from commodity manufacturers.

**How to use it:**
- Access the DSIR recognised R&D companies list from the official DSIR portal
- Filter by state (Gujarat) and sector (chemicals, pharmaceuticals, agrochemicals)
- Cross-reference against the revenue band using screener.in or MCA

**Expected yield:** High precision. Most DSIR-recognised companies in specialty chemicals will clear E1 and have strong C3 scores.

---

## Source 2 — BSE SME Platform

**What it is:** The BSE SME (Small and Medium Enterprises) platform lists smaller publicly traded companies that are too small for the main BSE board. Many specialty chemical companies in the Rs. 50–200 Cr revenue band are listed here.

**Why it helps:** BSE SME companies file annual reports, disclose revenue, name their decision-makers, and often disclose plant locations — providing the minimum evidence base needed for Federer scoring. This avoids the evidence gap that kills unlisted company evaluation.

**How to use it:**
- Filter BSE SME listings by sector code: Chemicals, Specialty Chemicals, Pharma Intermediates
- Filter by market cap or revenue range as proxy for the Rs. 50–500 Cr band
- Download annual reports directly for E1/E2 and scoring evidence

**Expected yield:** Medium precision, high volume. Expect significant triage needed to separate differentiated players from commodity manufacturers.

---

## Source 3 — MCA (Ministry of Corporate Affairs)

**What it is:** MCA provides financial filings for all registered Indian companies, including private unlisted entities. Tofler, Zauba Corp, and IndiaFilings provide structured access to MCA data.

**Why it helps:** Many strong Federer candidates in specialty chemicals are private, family-owned, and unlisted. They have no screener.in profile and no BSE filing. MCA is the only structured financial source for these companies.

**How to use it:**
- Search by NIC code (industrial classification) for specialty chemicals manufacturers in Gujarat
- Filter by registered address (Gujarat state) and company age (prefer 10+ years)
- Pull revenue from MCA filings via Tofler to apply revenue ceiling filter

**Limitation:** MCA data is typically 1–2 years delayed. Revenue figures require verification against more recent sources.

**Expected yield:** High volume, lower precision. Strong for discovering unlisted candidates; evidence quality will be thinner than listed company research.

---

## Source 4 — IndiaMART and B2B Chemical Directories

**What it is:** IndiaMART is India's largest B2B marketplace. It lists manufacturers by product category, location, and capacity. Chemical-specific directories include ChemicalsDirectory.com and IndiaBizDirectory.

**Why it helps:** Many sub-Rs. 200 Cr specialty chemical manufacturers are not listed on any exchange and have minimal web presence — but they maintain IndiaMART profiles to reach buyers. These profiles reveal product portfolios, manufacturing claims, certifications, and sometimes key contact information.

**How to use it:**
- Search by product type (e.g., "pharma intermediates Gujarat manufacturer", "custom synthesis Gujarat", "organolithium Gujarat")
- Filter to Gujarat suppliers only
- Use product specificity as a triage proxy — vague listings ("all types of chemicals") indicate commodity/trading; narrow, technical listings indicate specialty manufacturers
- Cross-reference claims against MCA filings

**Expected yield:** Medium volume, low-to-medium precision. Best used for discovery of small unlisted manufacturers that don't appear in any other source.

---

## Source 5 — Export Directories and DGFT Data

**What it is:** India's Directorate General of Foreign Trade (DGFT) and export platforms like Zauba.com, Import Genius, and Volza track actual shipment-level export data by exporter, product, and destination.

**Why it helps:** Export activity is one of the strongest signals for Federer company fit. Companies exporting to regulated markets (US, EU, Japan) have cleared buyer qualification audits, maintain documented quality systems, and demonstrate global-standard operations. Export data bypasses the need to infer — it shows actual commercial relationships.

**How to use it:**
- Search by HS code (Harmonised System) for specialty chemical categories exported from Gujarat
- Identify Gujarat-based exporters with consistent multi-year export volumes
- Cross-reference exporter names against MCA and company websites to validate E1 and gather scoring evidence

**Expected yield:** High precision for C7 (systems maturity) and C5 (tailwinds) scoring. Export volume and destination data are strong C6 (growth signal) proxies.

---

## Source 6 — Chemical Industry Associations

**What it is:** Indian chemical industry associations maintain member directories and event participation records.

Key associations:
- **CHEMEXCIL** — Basic Chemicals, Cosmetics and Dyes Export Promotion Council
- **FICCI Chemical Committee**
- **GCCI** — Gujarat Chamber of Commerce and Industry
- **Indian Chemical Council**
- **Pharmexcil** — for pharma intermediate exporters

**Why it helps:** Association membership correlates with a minimum level of operational legitimacy and industry engagement. Event speaker lists, conference participants, and award recipients are particularly high-signal — companies sending technical leaders to industry events are demonstrating both capability and outward-facing engagement.

**How to use it:**
- Download member directories filtered to Gujarat and specialty chemicals
- Review conference speaker lists and award winners from the last 3 years
- Award categories (export excellence, innovation, sustainability) are useful Federer proxies

**Expected yield:** Medium volume, medium-to-high precision. Particularly useful for identifying founder-operators who are actively engaged in the industry.

---

## Source 7 — LinkedIn (Targeted People Search)

**What it is:** LinkedIn is the most direct source for identifying decision-makers and verifying DM quality (C4) before outreach.

**Why it helps:** For Federer scoring, the quality of the decision-maker matters as much as the company profile. LinkedIn allows direct verification of:
- DM title and tenure
- Educational and professional background
- Content they publish (signals mindset and priorities)
- Company size and employee structure (a proxy for operational scale)
- Recent hires in key functions (systems, operations, quality) as a C6 signal

**How to use it:**
- Search for "CEO/MD + Gujarat + specialty chemicals / pharma intermediates / custom synthesis"
- Filter by company size (50–500 employees as proxy for target revenue band)
- Review DM activity: recent posts, article shares, conference mentions
- Use "Alumni" search on engineering colleges (IIT, NIT, BITS, M.S. University Vadodara) filtered to chemical/chemical engineering graduates running Gujarat-based companies

**Expected yield:** High quality for C4 validation. Low volume for new discovery (LinkedIn search has limitations), but very high value for enriching existing candidates.

## Source Prioritisation by Research Stage

| Stage | Best Sources | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Discovery | DSIR list, BSE SME, GIDC directories | Build initial universe |
| Revenue filter | Screener.in, MCA via Tofler | Apply Rs. 500 Cr ceiling |
| E1/E2 validation | Company websites, annual reports | Confirm producer + Gujarat presence |
| Federer scoring | Annual reports, BSE filings, company websites | Evidence for C3–C8 |
| DM identification | LinkedIn, company websites, annual reports | C4 and outreach personalisation |
| Export verification | Zauba.com, DGFT, IndiaMART | C5 and C6 evidence |
| QA verification | MCA, BSE filings, DSIR portal | Resolve flagged uncertainties |

## Quality Control in Sourcing

Regardless of source, three rules apply:

1. **Discovery ≠ qualification.** A company appearing in a directory or database does not mean it qualifies. Every candidate must still pass E1, E2, and minimum Federer scoring.

2. **Single-source claims get conservative scores.** If a product claim or revenue figure appears in only one source, it is treated as unverified and scored conservatively (or flagged in QA).

3. **Reject rather than guess.** If adequate evidence cannot be found for a company, it is rejected rather than scored optimistically. A false PASS wastes outreach resources more than a false FAIL wastes sourcing effort.

---

## Key Learning

The highest-quality Federer candidates consistently appeared in multiple independent sources. Companies that were visible in DSIR records, export databases, annual reports, and LinkedIn simultaneously generally produced the strongest scores and required the least QA effort. Single-source candidates — especially those visible only on IndiaMART or only via MCA — had the highest QA attrition rate and the highest rate of eventual rejection.

The practical implication for scale-up: prioritise sources that naturally concentrate multi-signal companies (DSIR list, BSE SME, export data) over sources that surface large volumes of single-signal candidates (IndiaMART alone, MCA alone). Fewer candidates with stronger evidence bases are more valuable than larger volumes that require repeated QA loops.
